Preliminary FPR disparity analysis shows that, across all these variables, some disparities exist. For instance, and as shown in the following table, male students are more subject to miscategorization, as well as non-displaced students, non-scholarship holders, etc… However, two variables stand out : indebted students, and students whose tuition fees aren't up to date. Depending on the model, from a quarter up to a third of indebted students are miscategorized, and from a third to two thirds of students with invalid tuition fees are miscategorized. To understand what's going on here, let's have a look at how our models make their decisions.
To know exactly how sure the models are that certain students are going to drop out, we displayed, for our socio-demographical variables, the probability density functions. Essentially, we are looking at the dropout probabilities that our models associate with every student, to see how sure they are about their decision. First, let’s have a look at some variables other than the financial ones.
Graph 1 — Displaced
Graph 2 — Scholarship Holder
Graph 3 — Gender
What we see on those graphs is that, except for the Multiple-Layer-Perceptron, all models miscategorized students they are relatively unsure with. A lot of the miscategorization disparities happen with students whose dropout probabilities are between 0.5 and 0.8. With a decision threshold set at 0.5, the models still categorize these students as dropouts, even if they are not utterly convinced they are in fact going to drop out. A simple solution to this problem is to raise the decision threshold to 0.8 to cut miscategorization, and allow for students to be labeled as “dropout” only when the probability is high.
However, life isn’t so bright and simple for our financial variables. Here are the same graphs we just saw, but respectively for the Debtor variable, and for the Tuition fees up to date variable.
Graph 4 — Debtor
Graph 5 — Tuition Fees Up To Date
What we see here is that not only do our models miscategorize financially unstable students a whole lot more than the regular students, but they are also very much sure about their predictions. Even with a higher decision threshold, students would still be miscategorized. To push this analysis a bit further, let’s have a look at the FPR decay rate for each model, which is the FPR value for different decision thresholds.
FPR Decay — Effect of raising the decision threshold (Gradient Boosting)
What appears here is that, except for the random forest model (for reasons we will see later on), the difference in FPR decay rates show that, even for higher decision thresholds, financially unstable students still get miscategorized 5 to ten times more than students with other characteristics.

Top 5 Predictors
What appears here is that the top five predictors are grade related variables for both semesters of term, followed by the state of tuition fees. Grades are positively correlated with not dropping out, same thing as having up to date tuition fees.

No, let's see how grades are distributed and correlated with our financial features between our training and testing dataset.

What we see here is that, especially for the "tuition fees" variable, students in the training set tend to have lower grades and lower course approval than the students in the testing set. Further correlation analysis supports this claim.

Essentially, models learn two correlations in the training phase : grades predict dropout, and financially unstable students tend to have lower grades and to drop out more consequently. However, in the testing set, those correlations are not present as much, hence the models punish students based on correlations learned in training that are not found in testing. This is less of the case for the random forest model because these types of models are built to overfit less on training data.
